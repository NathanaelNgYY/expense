Three ways to redesign the budget tracker.

Same data, same features, three different central objects. The shipped app answers "how much have I spent" with a ring and a stack of cards. Each direction below answers a sharper question instead, and lets that answer choose the layout. Screens use real July 2026 figures: S$1,200 income, S$763.77 spent, S$212.05 of buffer left.

iPhone, shown at 330px thumb reach, 44px targets no usage ring, no card grid

Receipt

The month as a printed ledger. Nothing is a card, nothing is a ring. Every figure sits on a rule where you can read it against every other figure. Built for the person who already knows what they spent and wants to check the arithmetic.

Signature

A column per day, drawn against a dashed allowance line at S$17.74. Two days broke the line in July. You see which ones before you read a single number.

Palette

chroma 0 throughout. Vermilion appears only where you went over.

Type

Martian Mono for every figure, Switzer for labels. Tabular numerals so columns align down the page.

Cost

Density is high. This is the direction that rewards a user who checks daily and punishes one who opens it monthly.

Envelope

Money as a level, not a percentage. Each category is an envelope and the apricot line is the cash still inside it. Savings and Investments are commitments, so they seal shut instead of filling up. Buffer sits at the bottom of the stack because that is where you reach when an envelope runs dry.

Signature

The fill level, which is the money still in the envelope. Others has drained to the floor and its line has gone dashed. That is what "drawn from buffer" looks like before you read the words.

Palette

Drenched. Aubergine carries 80% of the surface, apricot is only ever money.

Type

Cabinet Grotesk in 800 for names and figures, Switzer for the rest. One family would have been safer and duller.

Cost

Six envelopes fit one screen. A user with twelve custom categories scrolls, and the stack metaphor starts to strain.

Pulse

Time is the axis. A budget is not a bucket you drain, it is a rate you hold. Every transaction is one tick on the month, and the only question the home screen answers is whether you are running hot.

Signature

Ticks sized by amount, placed by day, read against a dashed ideal pace line. On 10 July the actual line sits S$186.37 above ideal, and the chip says so.

Palette

No red, no green. Saffron marks attention, chalk marks now.

Type

General Sans for prose, Fragment Mono for axes and small figures. The mono is a measuring instrument, not a terminal.

Cost

Pace hides totals. A user who wants to know "how much is left in Lunch" needs one more tap than in the other two.

What these actually cost to build

All three keep the current data model untouched. Categories, entries, buffer, the S$1,200 monthly income, and both iOS Shortcut ingest paths survive as they are. What changes is which computed value gets the largest type.

  • Receipt needs one new derived value: spend grouped by day, flexible categories only. Everything else already exists in compute.ts.
  • Envelope needs no new math at all. It is a rendering of categoryDeficits and bufferRemaining as a level rather than a bar.
  • Pulse needs a cumulative series and an ideal pace line, plus a per category run rate. That is the largest of the three, and the only one whose insights screen has real work behind it.

Pick one and I will build it against the real screens. Mixing is fair too: Envelope's home with Pulse's insights is a coherent app, Receipt's ledger with Envelope's stack is not.
